Silent Signs: Typing Patterns and Parkinson's Risk

This project aims to explore the relationship between individual’s typing trends and their risk of Parkinson’s disease (PD). By analyzing the way people type, we hope to identify those with higher risks of developing PD more easily.

About Parkinson's Disease

Parkinson’s disease is a movement disorder impacted by the nervous system. It causes neurons in the brain to become damaged and weakened which impacts lifestyle such as movement issues, tremors, stiffness, and impaired balances. Severe PD patients might have troubles with walking, talking, and completing other daily tasks. The damage in the neurons causes the affected individuals to have a decrease in dopamine. The decrease in dopamine is why the main treatment for PD, Levodopa (L-DOPA), attempts to replace dopamine although a major side effect is that it can cause paralysis or involuntary movement. Both symptoms of PD and the side effects of the treatment impacts movement, leading back to our project goal to see the interaction it has with typing. Research has implied that varying typing patterns can be an indicator of PD. By utilizing and analysing these patterns, we can hopefully identify those at risk and provide earlier interventions.

About Our Dataset

The original dataset was collected by the Massachusetts Institute of Technology (MIT) on 85 subjects from Madrid, Spain. The dataset contains keystroke logs from subjects both with and without PD. Typing data was collected on standardized equipment.

Besides the keylogged data (measuring the key pressed, hold time, etc.), finger tapping tests were also taken that measured the speed and rhythm of finger movements, providing additional insights of the likelihood of PD.

One of the main takeaway points of the dataset is also the Unified Parkinson's Disease Rating Scale (UPDRS) score. The score is based upon a questionnaire that subjects complete, that assesses subjects on motor and non-motor symptoms on a scale from 0-4. It provides a final score range from 0-108 with 0 being minimal chances of PD and 108 being higher risk.

Understanding Our Data Subjects:

Density Histogram Comparison of WPM

  • Density histogram of Word per Minute (WPM) separated by those affected by PD and those that are not
  • Parkinson data is displayed in blue while non-PD is displayed in orange
  • Although it is expected that there’d be a vast difference in WPM since PD impacts movement, but in reality looking at our data, there is not that drastic of a difference in typing speed, considering how similar the distributions appear.
  • One of the only noticeable differences is the extremities (maximum) in the data with the fastest subject being a patient without PD.
  • The maximum WPM in the dataset is approximately 257.2 which is very unexpected as even professional court reporters often only achieve around 225 WPM with specialized equipment and phonetic shorthands.

Regressing WPM on UPDRS

  • A scatter plot of those with and without PD and the relationship between their typing speed and their UPDRS scores.
  • It is shown that there is a slight negative trend in the regression line
  • Patients without PD has a much wider range compared to patients with PD
  • Although the regression line is generally negative, due to overlapping values, typing speed alone likely isn't sufficient for diagnosis, other metrics need to be taken into consideration.
  • From a classification perspective, we can identify that there is a clear linear division boundary in the UPDRS between the two groups that lingers around 6-7 (this is in consideration that the UPDRS score provided is from section 3 of the UDRS examination that focuses on motor movements).

Conclusion

Although the model shows a general relationship between typing speed and UPDRS score, there is no definitive threshold that is able to classify an individual’s health status solely based upon typing speed. Any predictions made should be considered speculative and used only for exploratory purposes, NOT for clinical evaluation. In conclusion, our project aims to explore the relationship between typing patterns and Parkinson's disease risk. By analyzing typing trends, we hope to identify individuals at higher risk of developing PD, enabling earlier interventions and better management of the disease.

We believe that this approach can provide valuable insights into the early detection of Parkinson's disease and contribute to improving the quality of life for those affected by it.
